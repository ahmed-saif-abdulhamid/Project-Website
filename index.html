SlaughtCure Project

Abstract

In Egypt, the agriculture sector faces significant challenges, ranging from urban encroachment to salinization, but most importantly, the usage of unconventional and untreated wastewater resources in irrigation. Poultry farms, especially Abu Fayad farm in El-Sinbillawin, contribute significantly to this problem, disposing 54,750 liters of wastewater and 3,898 liters of blood annually in nearby agricultural lands. These disposals pose severe risks to both lands and the surrounding vicinities.

Although there are some proposed field solutions for treating blood-mixed water, most of them are meant to serve hospitals, nursing homes, and pharmaceutical industries; never for local poultry farms or small scale use. The purpose of this study is to provide a solution that ensures a life long treatment approach, integrated with a feedback system that is interactive with the parameters concentration of the water sample, giving the poultry farm a clean, healthy disposals that aren't harmful to the soil and local environment.

Of the study findings, the prototype could achieve the three main design requirements, yielding a TDS reduction percentage of 59.09%, a turbidity reduction of 93.88%, a final pH of 6.55, and a power consumption of 45.5W per liter of sample. Consequently, the major conclusion is that the project proved successful and efficient in treating poultry farms' wastewater, further solving the grand challenge of Improving sources of clean water.

Methods

1. System Design and Planning: The project began with detailed planning to structure the prototype's workflow. A 3D simulation was created to visualize the design, as shown in Figure 2.

2. Base Structure Assembly: The prototype was built as a vertical tower of 10 flower-like plant pots. Containers for each treatment phase were fixed in descending order, with pipes and pumps regulating water flow.

3. Screening, DAF, Coagulation, and Flocculation Phases: A mesh layer was glued inside the first container for physical screening. In the second, an air pump with a 1mm nozzle created a Dissolved Air Flotation (DAF) system. A web of mesh and bandage layers filtered rising FOG and contaminants. For coagulation and flocculation, lemon, vinegar, and cactus cladodes SlaughtCure mucilageâ€”extracted, cleaned, and boiled in distilled waterâ€”were used as natural agents to enhance sediment removal.

4. Odor Elimination Phase: The third container was filled with layers of tourmaline, zeolite, calcite, activated carbon, sand, and cotton to filter remaining impurities and eliminate odors.

5. CDI and UV Chamber Design: CDI electrodes were made from pencil-extracted graphite, activated carbon, water, and gelatin, then heated at 80Â°C and dried overnight. Electrodes were fixed in the fourth container and powered by crocodile clips. A UV bulb and air pump ensured sterilization and stirring. A final container connected to the second one completed the cycle.

6. Feedback & Circuit Design: A 10A/12V power supply powered 5 pumps, 2 vacuum pumps, and 3 sensors (pH, TDS, turbidity), all linked to a Raspberry Pi and Arduino relays. This system was linked to a mobile app, in which its design was made by Figma, and converted into code by Android Studio Code.

Results

I. Parameters Concentration (Turbidity, TDS, and pH)

Over a 4-hour duration and through 5 continuous cycles, the system achieved significant reductions in key parameter concentrations. Turbidity decreased from 930 NTU to 5 NTU, TDS levels from 1078.3 ppm to 441.4 ppm, and pH increased from 5.71 to 6.55, as shown in Figure 9, successfully meeting the design requirements.

II. Consumed Power per Liter of Sample

The system, including the feedback system and pumps, consumed 45.5 Watts per liter of sample (one cycle). The components with the highest power consumption were the Air pump, with a consumption ranging from 1.5W to 3.0W, the UV light bulb, and the water pumps. III. Treatment Efficiencies

From an initial water sample of 1 liter, the output volume was 678 mL, resulting in a treated water recovery efficiency of 67%. Turbidity, TDS, and pH had removal efficiencies of 93.88%, 59.09%, and 85.6%, as shown in Table 3.

IV. Life Expectancy of The Prototype

The life expectancy of the prototype was calculated to be 13 cycles. After that, the electrodes would corrode, reducing the CDI efficiency, the organic filter (sand & gravel, etc.) would clog, and the pumps could also be clogged with lipids and sediments.

Conclusion

The SlaughtCure project successfully developed an effective solution for treating poultry farm wastewater, meeting all major design requirements. With high reduction efficiencies for turbidity (93.88%), TDS (59.09%), and improved pH levels (from 5.71 to 6.55), the system demonstrates considerable potential for implementation in small-scale poultry operations.

The integration of natural coagulants, physical filtration, capacitive deionization, and UV sterilization proved to be a comprehensive approach to treating blood-contaminated wastewater. With a moderate power consumption of 45.5W per liter and a volume recovery rate of 67.8%, the system strikes a balance between effectiveness and resource efficiency.

The project directly addresses the grand challenge of improving clean water sources in agricultural settings, providing a sustainable alternative to the current practice of untreated wastewater disposal. By focusing on local implementation and utilizing accessible materials, SlaughtCure represents a practical step toward mitigating water pollution from poultry operations.
